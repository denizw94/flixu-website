# What Is Cultural Localization?

> **Cultural Localization** is the process of adapting a product or campaign to fit the cultural norms, visual conventions, legal requirements, and social expectations of a target market — beyond translating the words. A product that has been culturally localized reads, looks, and behaves as though it was built for that market, not translated into it.

## How It Differs from Translation

[Localization](/topic/glossary/localization) covers the full adaptation of a product for a new market. Translation is one component of that — converting text from one language to another. Cultural localization is a different layer: it addresses the non-linguistic elements that shape whether something feels native or foreign.

A grammatically correct German translation can still feel off if it uses American business idioms, presents date formats in MM/DD/YYYY, or assumes a checkout flow that doesn't match German privacy expectations. These aren't translation errors. They're cultural misalignments that translation tools can't fix on their own.

## What Cultural Localization Covers

**Color and visual conventions.** Color associations differ by region. In most Western financial interfaces, red signals loss and green signals gain. In several East Asian markets, the convention is reversed. Using the wrong convention in a fintech dashboard doesn't just look wrong — it sends the opposite signal to the user. The same applies to imagery: a photo that reads as approachable and collaborative in a US context may read as informal in a market with different professional norms.

**Idiomatic language and [transcreation](/topic/glossary/transcreation).** Idioms tied to a specific cultural context — a sports metaphor, a regional expression, a pop culture reference — often don't survive literal translation. Transcreation replaces the expression with a locally equivalent one that carries the same emotional intent, rather than converting it word for word into something that means nothing to the audience.

**Formats, units, and regional conventions.** Date formats, number separators, currency symbols, measurement units, and address structures vary by locale. A form that asks a Brazilian user for a "Social Security Number" instead of a CPF will create confusion at the point of conversion. These adaptations are often handled by the [Cultural Adaptation Engine](/features/cultural-adaptation) — covering currencies, measurements, date formats, and timezone display.

**Legal and regulatory requirements.** Different markets operate under different legal frameworks. A sign-up flow in Germany needs to surface GDPR consent in a way that may not be required in a US-equivalent flow. A healthcare product entering a regulated market needs to adapt not just language but the structure of disclosures and consent language. These aren't optional — they're conditions of operating in those markets.

## Cultural Localization vs. Translation

| | Translation | Cultural Localization |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Text conversion | Text + visuals + UX + legal + formats |
| **Tool dependency** | MT or human translator | Requires market-specific knowledge |
| **What it fixes** | Language accuracy | Cultural fit and trust signals |
| **Detectable by users** | Grammatical errors | Subtle wrongness — something "feels off" |
| **When it matters most** | All localized content | Customer-facing products, marketing, regulated industries |

The practical distinction: translation handles what the words mean. Cultural localization handles what the product communicates. A product can be accurately translated and still feel obviously foreign to its target audience.

## Why It Matters for B2B Products

In consumer markets, a cultural misalignment might create friction. In B2B, it often creates distrust. When an enterprise buyer evaluates a software vendor, the product's behavior signals something about the vendor's understanding of their market. A product that uses the wrong date format in contracts, the wrong register in onboarding emails, or the wrong color convention in data visualizations signals that the vendor hasn't fully considered their context.

[Formality levels](/topic/glossary/formality-levels) sit at the intersection of language and culture. Whether a B2B product addresses users formally or informally isn't just a stylistic choice — it reflects an understanding of the professional norms in that market. Getting it wrong in German (_Sie_ vs. _du_) or Japanese (keigo levels) communicates something the vendor didn't intend.
